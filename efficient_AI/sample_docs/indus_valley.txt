The Indus Valley Civilisation was a Bronze Age civilisation in the
northwestern regions of South Asia, lasting from roughly 3300 BCE to 1300 BCE.
Its mature period is dated between 2600 BCE and 1900 BCE. Along with ancient
Egypt and Mesopotamia, it was one of three early cradles of civilisation.

The civilisation's cities were noted for their urban planning, baked brick
houses, elaborate drainage systems, water supply systems, and clusters of
large non-residential buildings. The two greatest cities, Mohenjo-daro and
Harappa, emerged around 2600 BCE along the Indus River valley. Mohenjo-daro
was one of the largest cities of the ancient world, with an estimated
population of around 40,000 people at its peak.

A sophisticated and technologically advanced urban culture is evident in the
Indus Valley Civilisation. The quality of municipal town planning suggests the
knowledge of urban planning and efficient municipal governments which placed a
high priority on hygiene. The streets of major cities were laid out in a
perfect grid pattern. Houses were protected from noise, odours, and thieves.

The Harappan people had knowledge of metallurgy and produced copper, bronze,
lead, and tin. They developed a system of standardised weights and measures.
The Indus script remains undeciphered, and as a result the language of the
civilisation is unknown. The decline of the civilisation around 1900 BCE is
often linked to climate change and shifting river patterns, though the exact
causes remain debated among historians.
